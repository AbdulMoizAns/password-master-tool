# The Top 10 Most Common Passwords of 2026 (And Why They Fail)

Every year, security researchers compile lists of the most commonly used passwords, and the results are both predictable and alarming. Despite decades of cybersecurity awareness campaigns, millions of people continue to use shockingly weak credentials. As we move through 2026, let's examine the top 10 most common passwords this year and understand precisely why each one fails spectacularly at providing any meaningful security.

## The State of Password Security in 2026

Before diving into the list, it's important to understand the context. In 2026:
- Data breaches have exposed over 30 billion accounts cumulatively
- AI-powered cracking tools can test trillions of combinations per second
- Password managers are more accessible than ever
- Multi-factor authentication is widely available

Yet, despite these advances and warnings, poor password habits persist. Let's explore why.

---

## #10: "Welcome123!"

### Why It's Popular
This password feels welcoming and includes the trifecta of complexity requirements: uppercase, numbers, and a symbol. Many corporate systems default to suggesting similar patterns during account creation.

### Why It Fails
- **Dictionary word**: "Welcome" is among the first words in every attack dictionary
- **Predictable pattern**: Word + sequential numbers + symbol is a well-known formula
- **High frequency**: Appears in millions of breached databases
- **Crack time**: Less than 1 second with modern tools

### The Reality
Attackers don't need sophisticated algorithms for this one—it's literally on their "try first" list. Using "Welcome123!" is like locking your door but leaving the key in the lock.

---

## #9: "Qwerty123456"

### Why It's Popular
The QWERTY pattern follows the keyboard layout, making it easy to type without looking. Adding "123456" feels like adding complexity.

### Why It Fails
- **Keyboard pattern**: Attackers specifically target keyboard walks (sequential keys)
- **Sequential numbers**: "123456" is the most common number sequence
- **No actual randomness**: Every character is predictable based on position
- **Crack time**: Instant—this is often the second password tried after "password"

### The Reality
Keyboard patterns provide zero entropy. This password offers no more security than having no password at all.

---

## #8: "Admin2026!"

### Why It's Popular
Many users incorporate the current year thinking it makes their password fresh and unique. "Admin" feels authoritative and professional.

### Why It Fails
- **Common base word**: "Admin" is heavily targeted, especially for work accounts
- **Year inclusion**: Attackers automatically try current and recent years
- **Predictable structure**: Word + Year + Symbol is a documented pattern
- **Crack time**: Under 5 seconds

### The Reality
Including the year doesn't make your password current—it makes it predictable. Attackers know people do this and adjust their dictionaries accordingly. Next year, "Admin2027!" will be just as vulnerable.

---

## #7: "Letmein123"

### Why It's Popular
Short, simple, and feels like a natural phrase. It's been popular for decades, and familiarity breeds continued use.

### Why It Fails
- **Historical infamy**: One of the oldest known weak passwords
- **Phrase-based**: "Let me in" is a common expression included in attack dictionaries
- **Minimal length**: Only 11 characters with low entropy
- **Crack time**: Less than 1 second

### The Reality
"Letmein" has been on breach lists since the 1990s. Using it in 2026 is digital archaeology—and not the good kind.

---

## #6: "Password2026"

### Why It's Popular
It literally says "password" with the current year appended. Some users think this is clever or ironic. Others simply don't realize how obvious it is.

### Why It Fails
- **The word "password"**: The single most targeted string in existence
- **Year suffix**: Automatically generated by cracking tools
- **Zero creativity**: No attempt at obscurity or randomness
- **Crack time**: Effectively instant

### The Reality
This is the equivalent of naming your Wi-Fi network "MyWiFi." It's so obvious that it bypasses intelligence entirely and goes straight to brute force—which it still fails instantly.

---

## #5: "1234567890"

### Why It's Popular
Pure convenience. It's the fastest password to type, requires no thought, and satisfies minimum length requirements on many systems.

### Why It Fails
- **Pure sequence**: Ten consecutive numbers offer minimal entropy
- **First instinct**: Among the very first combinations attackers try
- **Numeric only**: Limited character set reduces possibilities
- **Crack time**: Microseconds

### The Reality
This isn't a password; it's a placeholder that someone forgot to replace. Using numeric-only sequences is security theater at its worst.

---

## #4: "Il0veyou2026"

### Why It's Popular
Personal sentiment combined with the current year. Users feel emotionally connected to phrases about love, family, or pets.

### Why It Fails
- **Common phrase**: "I love you" variants are extensively cataloged
- **Leet speak substitution**: Replacing "o" with "0" is a well-known pattern
- **Social engineering**: Love-related passwords are easily guessed by people who know you
- **Crack time**: Under 10 seconds

### The Reality
Your romantic sentiments don't belong in your password. Beyond being crackable by strangers, these are often guessed by disgruntled partners, friends, or family members during conflicts.

---

## #3: "Monkey123"

### Why It's Popular
Random animal names feel unpredictable to users. "Monkey" has been a go-to "random" word for generations.

### Why It Fails
- **Top dictionary word**: "Monkey" consistently ranks in the top 50 most used password words
- **Number suffix**: "123" is the default addition
- **Cultural ubiquity**: Taught as an example of a "fun" password, making it universally known
- **Crack time**: Under 2 seconds

### The Reality
What feels random to humans is statistically predictable. "Monkey," "Dragon," "Tiger," and similar animal names are all heavily represented in attack dictionaries.

---

## #2: "12345678"

### Why It's Popular
Slightly longer than "123456," satisfying systems that require 8+ characters while maintaining maximum typing speed.

### Why It Fails
- **Sequential numbers**: Offers almost zero entropy despite 8 characters
- **Most common 8-character password**: Literally #1 in breached databases
- **No character diversity**: Numbers only
- **Crack time**: Instant

### The Reality
This password has held the top spot for most of the past two decades. Its continued popularity represents a collective failure of password education.

---

## #1: "Password123"

### Why It's Popular
It combines the most obvious word with the most obvious numbers. Users either don't care about security, don't know better, or prioritize convenience over protection.

### Why It Fails
- **The word "password"**: Maximum target priority
- **Sequential numbers**: Default suffix
- **Universal knowledge**: Everyone knows this is bad, yet millions use it
- **Crack time**: Zero seconds—it's pre-computed in every attack tool

### The Reality
"Password123" is the poster child for terrible password choices. It's so common that security professionals use it as a benchmark: if your password is weaker than or equal to this, you've failed at basic security.

---

## Why Do These Passwords Persist?

Understanding why these passwords remain popular despite widespread knowledge of their weakness reveals important psychological factors:

### 1. Convenience Over Security
Typing "12345678" takes less than a second. Creating and remembering a strong password requires effort. For many, convenience wins.

### 2. Optimism Bias
"It won't happen to me" thinking leads people to believe they're not targets worth attacking.

### 3. Lack of Immediate Consequences
Unlike running a red light, weak passwords don't produce immediate negative feedback. Breaches might not be discovered for months or years.

### 4. Complexity Fatigue
Constant password requirements across dozens of accounts lead to decision fatigue and fallback to simple options.

### 5. Misunderstanding Risk
Many users don't grasp the real-world consequences of compromised accounts until it's too late.

---

## The Real-World Impact

Using any of these top 10 passwords puts you at severe risk:

- **Account Takeover**: Attackers gain full access to your email, social media, and financial accounts
- **Identity Theft**: Personal information enables fraud across multiple platforms
- **Financial Loss**: Direct theft from bank accounts or unauthorized purchases
- **Privacy Violations**: Exposure of private messages, photos, and documents
- **Professional Damage**: Work accounts compromised can lead to job loss or legal liability
- **Cascade Effect**: One breached account leads to others through password reuse

---

## How to Protect Yourself

### Immediate Actions
1. **Check Your Passwords**: Use services like "Have I Been Pwned" to see if your credentials appear in breaches
2. **Change Weak Passwords**: Replace any password on this list immediately
3. **Enable 2FA**: Add multi-factor authentication wherever available
4. **Use a Password Manager**: Generate and store unique, strong passwords for every account

### Long-Term Strategies
1. **Adopt Passphrases**: Use 4+ random words for memorable yet secure credentials
2. **Never Reuse Passwords**: Each account deserves a unique password
3. **Regular Audits**: Review and update passwords quarterly
4. **Stay Informed**: Follow security news to understand emerging threats

---

## Conclusion

The top 10 most common passwords of 2026 represent a catastrophic failure of digital hygiene. Each one fails for obvious reasons: predictability, dictionary inclusion, keyboard patterns, or sequential characters. None provide meaningful protection against even amateur attackers, let alone sophisticated cybercriminals.

The persistence of these passwords isn't due to lack of information—we've known these are terrible for decades. It's a behavioral problem requiring better tools (password managers), better habits (passphrases), and better understanding of real-world risks.

Don't be a statistic. If you recognize any of these passwords in your own credential collection, change them today. Your digital security—and potentially your financial future—depends on making better choices than the millions who continue using these catastrophically weak passwords.

In 2026, there's no excuse for weak passwords. The tools, knowledge, and alternatives are all readily available. The only question is whether you'll take action before becoming the next breach headline.
